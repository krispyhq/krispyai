There's a specific kind of frustration that's different from just "bad support." It's when you've spent three messages explaining your situation to a bot, the bot finally agrees it can't help — and then an agent pops in with: "Hi! How can I help you today?"

You start over. Everything you said to the bot is gone.

That's not a handoff. That's a restart with extra steps. [Understanding why the model exists helps](/what-is-human-in-the-loop-live-chat), but this post is about preventing the restart.

## Where context actually lives (and where it disappears)

When a conversation happens in a typical live chat setup, the bot holds state in memory — short-lived, session-scoped, maybe logged somewhere. When the bot escalates, the "handoff" is often just: fire a notification to an agent, open a new ticket, attach whatever summary the bot managed to produce.

The visitor waits. The agent starts fresh. The friction is structural, not accidental.

Krispy takes a different approach. There's a single Cloudflare Durable Object per conversation that owns the entire thread — bot messages, visitor messages, agent replies — as a continuous ordered log. Nothing is summarized, nothing discarded. When an agent steps in, they step *into* the same object. So does the visitor's widget. One source of truth, two participants.

The [full architectural reasoning behind using a Durable Object here](/how-we-built-live-takeover-durable-objects) goes deeper than this post — but the practical upshot is simple: you can't drop context when the whole conversation lives in one place.

## What the visitor experiences

From the visitor's side, nothing visual should announce the handoff. The chat continues in the same window, the same thread. No loading spinner, no "connecting you to an agent" interstitial that signals the previous conversation is now in the past.

What they do see: the next message arrives with a different name and the reply pattern changes — less instant, more considered. That's enough. Visitors know they're talking to a human because the responses feel human, not because a banner told them.

What they *don't* need to do: repeat themselves. The agent already read the transcript.

## What the agent experiences

[Krispy routes handoffs to Telegram](/connect-telegram-handoff). When a handoff triggers, a notification arrives in your Telegram — or your team's group chat — with the visitor's last message, the full prior conversation, and a prompt to reply.

Replying to that Telegram message routes your answer back to the visitor's widget. No dashboard to open, no new tab to switch to.

The setup takes about two minutes, once:

```sh
wrangler secret put TELEGRAM_BOT_TOKEN
wrangler secret put TELEGRAM_CHAT_ID
```

That's the entire integration. Two env vars. The Durable Object handles delivery, ordering, and routing your reply to the right visitor even if three conversations are happening at once.

## When no one replies immediately

This is the case people don't think about until it breaks. What happens at 2am? What if you're on a flight?

The Durable Object keeps the conversation open indefinitely. The visitor's session doesn't expire in the widget while they're in the tab. If they close it and come back, the thread is still there.

You can configure a fallback message the bot sends after a timeout — something like "We're a small team and might be a few hours, but we will reply." That's honest, sets the right expectation, and doesn't abandon the conversation.

When you eventually open Telegram and reply — hours later, different timezone — the message delivers. If the visitor is in the tab, it arrives live. If not, it's waiting on their next visit. The bot doesn't pretend to be a human during the gap. It holds the queue and tells the truth.

## Triggering the handoff

Handoff triggers are configurable in `krispy.config.ts`. The defaults cover two cases:

1. **Explicit request** — visitor types "human," "agent," "real person," or anything on your keyword list.
2. **Confidence threshold** — if the bot produces a low-confidence answer two turns in a row, it escalates automatically before the visitor has to ask.

Both can run simultaneously. First trigger that fires wins. If you want to watch confidence scores in real time to calibrate without guessing:

```sh
wrangler tail
```

Actual scores and which turn triggered the escalation show up in the log stream. Useful for tuning the threshold to match the kind of questions your visitors actually ask.

## FAQ

### When the agent takes over, do the bot messages disappear from the visitor's view?

No. The entire transcript stays in the widget exactly as it happened — bot replies, visitor messages, agent replies, all in the same thread in order. The only visible change is the name on incoming messages. From the visitor's perspective, the conversation just continues. There's no visual reset and no moment where they can tell the handoff happened.

### How does the agent know what the visitor already said?

The Telegram handoff notification includes the actual prior conversation, not a summary. The agent reads it before they type a single word. This is the core guarantee: no "can you repeat your issue?" moments, because the full transcript is attached before the first reply. That one detail is what separates a real handoff from a restart.

### Can the handoff trigger automatically without the visitor asking?

Yes. Krispy tracks bot confidence per turn. If the bot falls below your configured threshold twice in a row, it escalates and notifies you in Telegram without the visitor needing to ask. This is the "graceful exit before things go sideways" path — the bot hands off before it's visibly failing rather than after it's already frustrated someone.

### What if no one replies and the visitor closes the tab?

The conversation stays open in the Durable Object. When an agent eventually replies in Telegram, the message is waiting in the thread for the visitor's next visit. You can also configure a bot fallback — an honest "we'll get back to you in a few hours" — so the visitor doesn't wonder if their message went nowhere. Nothing expires on a timer.

### Does this work for a solo founder who checks Telegram once an hour?

That's actually the main use case. The bot handles first contact instantly, which covers most visitors without any human involvement. For the ones who genuinely need a person, you get a Telegram ping, reply when you can, and it reaches them. You're not running a 24/7 support desk — you're building a human fallback that works at the pace a small team can actually sustain.
