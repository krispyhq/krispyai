Most teams land on a paid live chat platform the same way: a free trial that became a habit, a Slack message that went "what is this $340 invoice?", and a slow realization that three years of customer conversations sit in a database they don't control.

That's the real reason to look at open-source live chat in 2026. Not ideology. Just: who owns the data, who controls the cost, and what happens when you want to leave.

## What "open-source" actually means here

Worth a quick calibration before the list. Some products call themselves open source but publish source-available code with licenses that ban commercial self-hosting. Others are genuinely MIT. A few are free-tier SaaS wearing an open-source hat.

**Chatwoot** is the benchmark — genuinely MIT-licensed, multi-channel, proven in production across thousands of installs. No AI built-in; you'd wire your own. If you're weighing the two side by side, [we've done that comparison in detail](/krispy-vs-chatwoot).

**Rocket.Chat** is the enterprise sibling: open-source community edition, rich feature set, but it wants a real server, MongoDB, and Redis. Better suited to a support team of 10 than a solo founder shipping at midnight.

**Papercups** was a clean Elixir-based option that never quite reached escape velocity. The hosted cloud shut down; the self-hosted project is quiet. Worth a fork if you love Elixir, otherwise skip it.

**Tawk.to** is free SaaS, not open source — you can't self-host it or read the code. [The trade-off is business-model shaped](/krispy-vs-tawk): they give you the widget for free and monetize through agent outsourcing and upsells. Your conversation history lives on their servers.

**Crisp** is similarly not open source. The Community plan is free and partially self-hostable but strips most of the features that make Crisp worth using. Paid plans run $25–$95/seat/month. [We compared them directly here](/krispy-vs-crisp).

**Intercom** is fully closed, $74–$139/seat/month for plans with real AI. Powerful, obviously. [The cost math compounds fast](/krispy-vs-intercom) once you have more than a couple of seats.

## FAQ

### What is the best free open-source live chat in 2026?

Chatwoot and Krispy are the two strongest free, genuinely MIT-licensed options. Chatwoot is more mature with broader channel integrations. Krispy is lighter, edge-native, and includes AI replies and Telegram handoff without any additional setup. Both are free to self-host with no per-seat fees.

### Is Tawk.to actually open source?

No. Tawk.to is free SaaS — you can't self-host it or audit the source. The business model is agent outsourcing: the widget is free because they sell human support agents as an upsell, and your conversation data lives on their infrastructure.

### Can I self-host Intercom or Crisp?

Intercom is closed-source with no self-hosting option. Crisp has a Community edition you can self-host, but it removes most of the features that justify using Crisp in the first place. If full-featured self-hosting matters, Chatwoot or Krispy are cleaner choices.

### How hard is it to deploy Krispy on Cloudflare Workers?

Straightforward if you're comfortable with a terminal. Clone the repo, set your Telegram bot token and chat ID as environment variables, run `npx wrangler deploy`. Workers AI is wired in automatically — no external API key needed. The README has the full walkthrough.

### Does open-source live chat support AI replies in 2026?

Chatwoot and Rocket.Chat support AI via plugins or webhooks — you bring your own provider and key. Krispy has AI replies built in via Cloudflare Workers AI, so there's no separate key or billing to set up. Intercom's Fin AI is the most polished paid option, but it's priced accordingly.
