Tawk.to's pitch is hard to argue with on paper: fully functional live chat, forever free. No trial, no credit card. Just paste the widget script and go. Thousands of small teams pick it for exactly that reason, and it works. But "free" in live chat software almost always means someone else is getting something out of the deal — and with tawk.to, the catches are specific enough to be worth knowing before you embed their script.

## What "free" actually costs you

The tawk.to widget ships with a "Powered by tawk.to" badge by default. Removing it costs $19/month. That's the first catch: your users see their branding on your product until you pay.

The second catch shows up when you want AI. Tawk.to's AI Assist — the feature that drafts replies and answers questions automatically — is a paid add-on. So the thing that makes live chat worth having in 2026 isn't included in the free plan.

The third catch is structural: tawk.to is closed-source SaaS. Your conversation history, customer data, and chat transcripts live on their servers. You can export, but you can't run it yourself, audit the code, or leave cleanly.

None of this is buried in fine print. It's their business model, and a reasonable one. But if you're evaluating free live chat, you should know what's in the box.

## How Krispy compares

Krispy starts from a different premise. The software is open source, and the default deployment runs on Cloudflare Workers — so you self-host at zero marginal cost. The AI is Workers AI, which means no separate API key to purchase. When the bot can't handle something and hands off to a human, the message goes to Telegram — your phone, the one you already have open.

The honest comparison: tawk.to's mobile app for human agents is polished and has been through years of iteration. Krispy's Telegram handoff is lighter — no queue routing, no internal inbox, no read receipts between agents. If you're managing five agents juggling parallel conversations, tawk.to (or [Crisp, which we compared separately](/krispy-vs-crisp)) might be the more appropriate choice. If you're a solo founder or a small team that wants to own the stack and reply from Telegram like a normal person, the calculus flips.

## Deploying Krispy takes one command

After cloning the repo and setting two secrets, the full deploy is:

```bash
wrangler secret put TELEGRAM_BOT_TOKEN
wrangler secret put TELEGRAM_CHAT_ID
npx wrangler deploy
```

That's it. There's no third-party account to configure, no dashboard to navigate, no badge to pay to remove. The Worker that runs your chat widget is your Worker, on your Cloudflare account, subject to Cloudflare's free tier — which covers tens of millions of requests a day for most products.

If you'd rather not manage infrastructure at all, [Krispy Cloud](https://krispy.ai) has a free tier. Same widget, same Telegram handoff, same zero forced branding — just hosted.

## When tawk.to is the right call

If your team is large enough to need queue routing, shift scheduling, and a shared inbox with internal notes — tawk.to is genuinely good at that. It's been around since 2013, the product is stable, and $19/month per agent is less than what most teams spend on Slack. There's no shame in paying for a tool that fits.

Where it stops fitting: if you're building a product on open-source principles, if you want conversation data on infrastructure you control, or if you're already reading [the best open-source live chat alternatives in 2026](/best-open-source-live-chat) and want something you can fork. Closed-source SaaS isn't an architecture choice you can undo later.

The reason Krispy exists at all comes from that ceiling. We kept hitting it — paid plans, hosted data, branding we didn't own. The full version is in [why we made Krispy open source](/why-open-source), but the short version is: we built the thing we wanted to use.

## FAQ

### Is there a free live chat alternative to tawk.to that doesn't show a powered-by badge?

Yes. Krispy is open source and self-hostable on Cloudflare Workers — no badge, no vendor branding, no per-seat charge. Krispy Cloud's free tier is also badge-free. With tawk.to, removing the "Powered by tawk.to" badge costs $19 per agent per month, which is the main thing people discover after they've already embedded the widget.

### Does tawk.to have AI built into its free plan?

No. AI Assist is a paid add-on for tawk.to, not included in the free tier. Krispy bundles Workers AI out of the box — the bot can answer questions automatically without a separate API key or paid subscription.

### Can I self-host tawk.to on my own servers?

No. Tawk.to is closed-source SaaS with no self-hosted option. Krispy is MIT-licensed and designed to run on Cloudflare Workers. You own the deployment, the data stays in your account, and the code is auditable.

### How does Krispy's human handoff work compared to tawk.to's?

When Krispy's AI can't answer — or a user asks to speak to a person — Krispy routes the conversation to a Telegram message. You reply in Telegram and the customer sees your reply in the chat widget. Tawk.to's handoff routes to their own mobile app, which has a more fully-featured agent inbox. Krispy's Telegram approach trades inbox features for zero new tools to install.

### Is Krispy actually free, or is there a catch like tawk.to?

Self-hosted Krispy is genuinely free. Cloudflare Workers' free tier handles millions of requests per day, and Workers AI has its own free quota. For most small-to-medium products, the real cost is zero. Krispy Cloud has a free tier too. The project is MIT-licensed — you can read the source, fork it, and run it however you like.
