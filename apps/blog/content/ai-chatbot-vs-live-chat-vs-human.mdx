Most founders don't actually need to choose between AI and humans. They need to stop paying $300/month for a tool that does both badly.

Here's the real question: **at what point in a conversation does a bot make your visitor feel heard, and at what point does it make them feel trapped?** Get that boundary right, and everything else follows.

## Three modes, three jobs

**Pure AI chatbot** — answers autonomously, no escalation path. When it doesn't know, it stops. Good for FAQ bots, lead capture, booking. The visitor either gets what they need or they bounce.

**Human live chat** — a real person monitors a dashboard and replies in real time. Expensive at scale, but nothing beats it for nuance in high-stakes conversations.

**Human-in-the-loop (HITL)** — a bot handles the conversation until it can't, then hands off to a human with full context. [The key is that the handoff is structured](/what-is-human-in-the-loop-live-chat): the human sees the history and continues seamlessly. The visitor doesn't restart.

HITL isn't a compromise. For most early-stage products, it's the only architecture that scales.

## When a pure bot is enough

Bots work when the answer space is bounded. If 80% of your questions are "what's your refund policy" or "how do I reset my password", a well-prompted bot handles all of them at any hour with no seat fee. Let the bot cook 🥐.

The failure mode: a visitor has a real problem — billing dispute, a bug they've hit twice — and the bot loops them through the same three answers. Without an escape hatch to a human, the bot becomes a wall.

## When you actually need a live human

Some conversations require a person from the first message: pre-sales on large contracts, a customer whose data got corrupted, anything where trust is the product being sold.

The cost of staffing this is real. A single agent covering standard hours runs $3,000–$5,000/month before tooling. Enterprise live chat platforms add another $74–$139/seat on top. That math doesn't work for a 3-person team.

If you genuinely need a person in every conversation, own that. But most teams who think they need full human coverage actually need a bot that escalates well.

## The case for human-in-the-loop

HITL is the practical middle. The bot handles the predictable 70–80%. For the rest, it hands off to a human — not by dumping the visitor into a new queue, but by notifying the human with full conversation context so they can pick up mid-thread.

What makes this work in practice is the handoff channel. If your human is checking a support dashboard every 20 minutes, the "live" in live chat is a fiction. The human needs to be notified *immediately*, on a channel they're already watching.

## Choosing your mode

A few honest heuristics:

**Use a pure bot** if your support questions are repetitive, your docs already answer them, and your users are tolerant of async (e.g., a developer tool).

**Use full human live chat** if you're in a high-trust, high-ticket category — legal, finance, medical, enterprise sales — where the conversion value justifies the cost.

**Use human-in-the-loop** if you're somewhere in between, which is most products. You want AI to handle the load, but you want a human available when it matters. This is the right default for indie hackers, small SaaS teams, and anyone burned by a bot-only widget that turned an interested visitor into a support ticket.

## FAQ

**What's the main difference between an AI chatbot and a human-in-the-loop system?**

A pure AI chatbot answers autonomously with no fallback. Human-in-the-loop adds a structured escalation: when the AI can't handle a message, a human is notified with full conversation context and steps in. The visitor never restarts from zero.

**Can a small team realistically run human-in-the-loop live chat?**

Yes — that's the point. Tools like Krispy route escalations to Telegram, so there's no dashboard to staff. One person with a phone can handle the human side of HITL for moderate chat volume without it becoming a second job.

**Is human-in-the-loop better than a pure AI chatbot for conversion?**

For most products, yes. Pure bots deflect well but hurt trust on complex questions. The escape hatch to a human — even rarely used — signals there's a real person behind the widget, which improves confidence at the point of sale.

**What does human-in-the-loop cost compared to full live chat?**

Full live chat is expensive: agent time plus $25–$139/seat/month for the platform. HITL shifts most load to AI; you pay for human time only on escalations. Self-hosted Krispy costs $0 for the platform; Krispy Cloud has a free tier for low volume.

**When should I add human-in-the-loop to my AI chatbot?**

When visitors ask follow-up questions the bot can't handle, drop off after bot replies on pricing, or email you to say "your chat wasn't helpful." These patterns mean the bot's answer space is exhausted — a human in the loop would have kept those conversations alive.
